Graphic Design & Merch Management

Role: Management · Graphic Design

Overview

This project focuses on designing, producing, and managing physical merchandise using digital workflows. It combines vector-based graphic design with CNC-ready file preparation, vendor coordination, and basic operations management to run a small, game- adjacent merch line.

The work sits at the intersection of design, fabrication, and logistics: turning ideas into production-ready files, collaborating with manufacturers, and ensuring the final products are consistent, durable, and aligned with the intended aesthetic.

Goals & Constraints

Key goals for the project include:

  • Creating visually clear and appealing designs that survive physical production,
  • Ensuring files are accurate for CNC cutting and layered manufacturing,
  • Keeping costs, turnaround times, and minimum order quantities manageable, and
  • Maintaining a consistent “brand feel” across multiple items.

Practical constraints such as kerf tolerances, material thickness, shipping costs, and vendor capabilities heavily influence both design and decision-making.

Design Workflow

The design process starts digitally and moves toward fabrication-ready assets. Typical workflow:

  1. Concept sketching: rough visual ideas and references based on game aesthetics, characters, or motifs.
  2. Vector illustration: recreating and refining concepts in vector form for clean scaling, color separation, and precise outlines.
  3. Production preparation: separating art into layers, assigning stroke/line types for cutting vs. engraving, and ensuring paths are closed and manufacturable.
  4. Mockups and revisions: generating digital mockups to visualize final products and iterating based on feedback or constraints.

Careful line weight, spacing, and shape decisions are required so that small details remain legible after cutting, printing, or layering.

CNC & File Preparation

Since the project relies on CNC-style processes and layered acrylic or similar materials, files must be prepared with fabrication in mind:

  • Defining cut paths, engrave areas, and registration marks in separate layers,
  • Adjusting designs for kerf (material removed by the cutting tool) and tolerances so that pieces fit together cleanly,
  • Exporting in vendor-compatible formats (e.g., SVG, AI, or DXF) with clear color coding and naming conventions, and
  • Documenting dimensions and layer order for consistent replication.

This step translates aesthetic decisions into precise technical instructions a machine and vendor can follow.

Operations & Management

Beyond design, the project also involves managing the small-scale operations required to get merchandise produced and into people’s hands. This includes:

  • Communicating with vendors about specs, limitations, and sample runs,
  • Comparing pricing, minimum order quantities, and shipping options,
  • Tracking orders, timelines, and quality issues, and
  • Planning which designs are viable to produce within budget and time constraints.

This management layer is where design, economics, and logistics meet, and decisions here directly affect what can realistically be offered.

Brand & Visual Consistency

A recurring focus of the work is maintaining a coherent visual identity across items. Even when designs are inspired by different games or references, they are:

  • Aligned in terms of color usage and contrast,
  • Structured with similar line language and shape language, and
  • Presented in a way that feels part of the same collection.

This consistency helps pieces feel like they belong to a single designer and brand, not just a random assortment of items.

My Contributions

In this project, my responsibilities include:

  • Designing vector artwork and preparing CNC-compatible files,
  • Defining layer structures, cut lines, and tolerances for production,
  • Communicating with vendors and iterating based on samples or feedback, and
  • Organizing the pipeline from initial concept to final manufactured piece.

This work blends creative decision-making with very practical, constraint-driven problem-solving.

Status & Future Work

The project has resulted in a small but growing collection of merch designs that are ready for or already in production. Future directions include:

  • Expanding the product line with new designs and form factors,
  • Refining file standards and templates to speed up new item creation, and
  • Connecting the merch more directly with interactive or game-based projects.
